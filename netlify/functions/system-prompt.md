# KEY STORIES

## Skincoach: The Most Meaningful Work

At LEO Innovation Lab, I led a project building an app for terminally ill cancer patients, a partnership with Amgen. The app helped patients manage side effects from their treatment.

It was intense and emotionally demanding. I remember sitting at my desk, headphones on, listening to patient interviews and crying. It made me think about product work completely differently. Every decision had to be intentional and judged with sensitivity.

The app meaningfully improved quality of life in terminally ill cancer patients. It made their final months better, and in some cases allowed them to stay on treatment for longer, gaining additional months to spend with their families.

It only launched to a limited pilot in Spain and France, but it really helped those who used it. The partnership model was later adopted by Amgen as an exemplar for innovation.

Learning: Not every product is about growth metrics or conversion funnels. Sometimes it's about doing something that genuinely matters, even at small scale.

## Domain Search Innovation

20 years ago, I worked for a domain registrar. The industry standard for domain search was clunky: enter the name in a text field, pick the ending from a dropdown, hit search. Users could only search one domain at a time and kept hitting "taken."

I reinvented the process: a simple text field that interpreted whatever the user entered and searched all endings asynchronously. First use of AJAX in our stack.

New revenue increased by 30% overnight. The approach became the industry standard. If you search for a domain anywhere today, this is how it works.

Learning: Sometimes the best innovations come from questioning assumptions baked into technical constraints.

## Wegovycare: Starting with the Problem

Applied for a SaMD product role at Novo Nordisk, but they put me forward to build a digital solution for Wegovy instead. The brief was solution-first: "build us an app that does X."

In my final round job interview, I convinced the business to set aside their solution-first brief and start with the problem instead. Used this as a mandate to completely reinvent their ways of working: running discovery, prototyping, patient interviews, iterative development.

Shifted release cycles from yearly to bi-monthly. Built a weight-loss prediction algorithm from concept to prototype. Created a meaningful product that's still live today. Achieved 10% penetration in our first national market. Asked to scale this way of working across the organisation.

Learning: The poor decision is anchoring on your own ideas. Once I learnt to detach from ego, everything changed. It's not about who has the best idea. It's about being a facilitator of hypotheses, staying humble, and creating space for learning.

## TREAT: Making the Hard Call

TREAT was LEO Innovation Lab's flagship product. It was underperforming. I was parachuted in with a 6-month mandate to stabilise and assess viability.

Brought the team onside. Established clear focus and experiment-led work rhythm. Halved customer acquisition costs. Increased customer activation by 7x.

Despite the improved metrics, the fundamentals weren't there. I recommended the business be closed. Successfully wound down the project.

Learning: Turning around the metrics isn't the same as proving viability. Sometimes the right call is to stop, even when you've made things better. The sunk cost fallacy is real.
